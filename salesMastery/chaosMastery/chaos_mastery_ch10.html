The Silent Handover
Third-Party Platforms Hijack Your Customer Relationships
Every order placed through DoorDash, Uber Eats, or Grubhub generates a customer record. It includes the guest's name, phone number, email address, delivery address, and order history. That record belongs to the platform — not to the restaurant that cooked the food.

The Restaurant Business Online's documentation of the data ownership debate is explicit: "Data ownership has been one of the major sticking points between establishments and delivery providers. establishments say lack of access to customer information allows the companies to get between them and their guests." Joe Guszkowski, "NYC bill would require delivery companies to share data with establishments," Restaurant Business Online, May 14, 2021, restaurantbusinessonline.com

The DoorDash spokesperson's response in that same article makes the platform's position equally explicit: "DoorDash is committed to protecting user data and privacy and cannot support efforts to force delivery platforms to share customer data without customer consent." The data lives with DoorDash. DoorDash spokesperson, quoted in Restaurant Business Online, restaurantbusinessonline.com, May 2021

The Market Share Math

As of 2025, DoorDash held approximately 67% of the US delivery market share, with Uber Eats trailing at 23%, according to digital restaurant operations firm Deliverect — data cited in CNBC's reporting on the restaurant reservation wars. That means on a standard delivery channel, the platform that owns your customer data is the one processing two-thirds of every delivery order your venue fulfills. You cook the food. They own the customer. Deliverect, cited in CNBC, "Restaurant reservation wars heat up," Feb. 25, 2026

The flywheel running against you: CherryBerry RMS's analysis of third-party delivery impact documents the structural trap: "establishments lose ownership of customer data and relationships, making it impossible to build loyalty programs or conduct direct marketing." Without data, there is no loyalty program. Without a loyalty program, there is no retention tool. Without retention, repeat visit revenue — which once drove 70% of revenue for most independent operators — migrates to platforms that can re-target those same guests with competitor offers. CherryBerry RMS, "Third-Party Delivery Apps Are Destroying Your Restaurant," Medium, Nov. 20, 2025

The Financial Damage
What Platform Dependency Costs — Per Guest, Per Year
The commission line on the invoice is visible. The cost of not being able to re-market to those guests next month is not. The true cost of platform dependency is not 15–35% per order. It is the compounding lifetime value surrendered with every transaction processed through a channel you don't control.
The Commission Math

CherryBerry RMS's analysis establishes the base economics: "DoorDash charges establishments commission fees of 15–35% per order, which eliminates profit margins for most establishments that typically operate on only 3–5% margins." The analysis goes further: "Many establishments operate at a loss on delivery orders through Uber Eats. When a platform takes 30% commission on a $50 order ($15), and the restaurant must cover food costs (28–35%), labour (30–35%), and other expenses, the math results in negative profitability." CherryBerry RMS, Medium, Nov. 20, 2025

This is the visible cost. The invisible cost is what happens next. The guest who just ordered through DoorDash is now in DoorDash's CRM, not yours. DoorDash can re-target them with a competitor's promotion next week. You cannot contact them at all.

The Retention Destruction

Restaurant Business Online's Joe Guszkowski — reporting on 2025 restaurant technology — documents the conversion problem directly: "We continued to hear some of the same complaints from establishments about how hard it is to convert third-party delivery customers to first-party channels. Some operators have now given up entirely on converting people, and are instead treating third- and first-party customers as totally separate audiences." Joe Guszkowski, Restaurant Business Online, Nov. 24, 2025, restaurantbusinessonline.com

The operators who have "given up on converting" have accepted a permanent structural disadvantage: they are spending marketing dollars to acquire customers who will continue to re-order through the platform that owns the relationship. The platform earns the commission on every repeat visit. The operator earns the margin. The margin on a delivery order through a 30% commission platform is, mathematically, often nothing.

The cold acquisition multiplier: Without an owned email or SMS list, every marketing dollar spent is spent on cold acquisition — reaching people who have never ordered before. Cold acquisition costs 5x more than retention marketing to repeat guests. Operators without first-party data lists are permanently trapped in the more expensive half of the marketing funnel.

The Legislative Fight
Cities Are Fighting for Your Data — And What It Means
The data ownership battle between establishments and platforms has become a legislative fight. Cities are proposing laws that would require platforms to share guest data with the establishments that fulfill their orders. The platforms are fighting back. While the legal battle plays out, operators need a strategy that doesn't depend on a legislative win.
The NYC Legislation

Restaurant Business Online documented the data ownership fight directly: "A bill introduced in the New York City Council would require third-party delivery companies like DoorDash, Uber Eats and Grubhub to share more data with the establishments that use their marketplaces — including a customer's name, phone number, email address, delivery address and the contents of the order." The New York City Hospitality Alliance supported the bill. Joe Guszkowski, Restaurant Business Online, May 14, 2021, restaurantbusinessonline.com

DoorDash's Response — The Privacy Shield Argument

DoorDash's position — articulated in their response to the NYC bill — is the playbook every major platform uses to defend data control: "DoorDash is committed to protecting user data and privacy and cannot support efforts to force delivery platforms to share customer data without customer consent." The privacy framing transforms a commercial data monopoly argument into a consumer protection argument — and it has been effective in slowing legislative progress. DoorDash spokesperson, quoted in Restaurant Business Online, restaurantbusinessonline.com

The Independent Restaurant Coalition has gone further, calling for federal regulation: "Third-party platforms impose unsustainable financial burdens on independent establishments" through commission fees and data control structures that prevent operators from building independent guest relationships. Independent Restaurant Coalition, independentrestaurantcoalition.com, 2025

What Operators Can't Wait For

Grubhub's response to the NYC bill reveals the market movement happening regardless of legislation: the platform launched Grubhub Direct — allowing independent establishments to create their own ordering websites and capture customer data from those orders. The spokesperson's statement: "We believe in the principle that establishments should have the data to build relationships with their customers." Grubhub spokesperson, quoted in Restaurant Business Online, restaurantbusinessonline.com

The practical implication: The platforms that allow data access are doing so through their own products — DoorDash Drive, Storefront, and Self-Delivery; Grubhub Direct — which require separate agreements and redirect more of the relationship back to the platform anyway. The only channel that genuinely returns data ownership to the operator is first-party ordering through the operator's own domain.

The Reclaim Strategy
the Power of Direct Channels That Return Data to You
The data ownership battle can't wait for legislation. It has to be won at the transaction level — by building the direct channels that capture guest information at the moment of engagement, before it flows through a platform that will keep it.
First-Party Ordering — The Highest-Leverage Move

Qu's 2025 State of Digital Report — drawing from 170 brands across 85,000 locations — establishes the market directly: 40% of brands now identify first-party digital sales as their biggest revenue growth opportunity in 2025. For QSR brands specifically, that number rises to 55%. The logic is simple: first-party ordering means the brand owns the guest data, the relationship, and the full margin — no commission, no platform intermediary, no competitor ad served alongside the confirmation email. Qu 2025 State of Digital Report, qubeyond.com · 170 brands, 85,000 locations

The QR Code Collection Loop

The highest-converting real-world data capture mechanism for in-venue operators is the QR code opt-in at point of transaction. When a guest pays their tab, scans for a menu, or closes out at the bar, the QR interaction captures an email or phone number in exchange for a discount on the next visit, a loyalty enrollment, or access to a members-only perk. This is the "text every tab closeout for opt-in" model — and it works because the moment of transaction is the moment of highest engagement.

The Loyalty Re-Investment

Qu's data documents a market-wide pivot: operators are shifting budget from traditional loyalty programs (which require data they don't have) to guest data platforms — investments that build the underlying owned data infrastructure. Guest data platform spending rose 11% year-over-year in 2025 while traditional loyalty spending dropped 8%. The market is buying the foundation before the building. Qu 2025 State of Digital Report, qubeyond.com

The Intouch Insight finding: Their 2025 Third-Party Delivery Study — based on 600 mystery shops across the US from April to June 2025 — found that first-party channels showed stronger control over order customization and the guest relationship. The report notes: "First-party channels showed that operators are serious about owning the relationship." Intouch Insight, "Are the 2025 Third-Party Delivery Trends a Warning Sign for Operators?" intouchinsight.com, Jan. 8, 2026

The Lifetime Value Calculation
Customer Data as An Advantage — The power of LTV
Location used to be the competitive advantage. Being on a busy corner, near a stadium, on the right downtown block — those positions guaranteed traffic. In the app era, location is still important. But customer data has replaced it as the primary driver of repeat revenue. The operator who owns the relationship doesn't need the foot traffic — they can create it.
The LTV Equation

A guest who visits once per month, spends $45 per visit, for 2 years has a lifetime value of $1,080. The same guest who receives a well-timed re-engagement message — "Your favorite table is available Thursday" — and visits 1.3x per month instead has a lifetime value of $1,404. The difference is not the guest's loyalty. It is the operator's ability to communicate with them.

Qu's 2025 State of Digital Report documents the revenue impact concretely: "Even one extra visit per guest each year can boost revenue by 5 to 10%." That calculation — cited from Blaze Pizza CTO Chris Demery — was made in the context of unified data enabling personalized communication. Without owned data, that visit never gets triggered. The guest who would have responded to "we miss you" never receives the message. Chris Demery, CTO Blaze Pizza, via Qu 2025 State of Digital Report, qubeyond.com · MURTEC 2025

The Retention Advantage

The data ownership advantage is most powerful not in mass email blasts but in precise moments: the birthday message sent 3 days before the guest's birthday, the "you haven't visited in 45 days" re-engagement trigger, the "we just launched your favorite item in a new preparation" notification. None of these are possible without the guest's contact information and purchase history in a system you control.

The 5x acquisition cost multiplier: Without a list, every new guest is a cold acquisition. Cold acquisition costs 5x more than retaining a previous client — an email to an opted-in guest who has already visited twice costs a fraction of the cost of a social media ad. The operator who doesn't own their data is permanently running the more expensive version of their marketing spend.

The Independent Operator Response
How To Gain Data Ownership
The data ownership battle is not only being fought by lawyers and legislators. It is being fought every shift by independent operators who are making deliberate choices about which channels they build for — and which guests they own. The first-party movement is not theoretical. It is operational.
The Blackbird Labs Model

Ben Leventhal — Resy and Eater founder — responded to the platform consolidation trend not by lobbying or litigating but by building: Blackbird Labs, a loyalty program specifically designed to return data ownership to independent establishments. The company, founded in 2022, allows independent establishments to capture guest data through a loyalty layer that the restaurant controls, not the platform. Ben Leventhal, cited in CNBC, "Restaurant reservation wars heat up," Feb. 25, 2026

Leventhal's founding logic reflects the same insight this chapter opens with: in a market where DoorDash, Uber Eats, and AmEx are all racing to control the dining relationship, the independent operator's best defense is owning the relationship independently — through tools designed specifically to return data sovereignty to the restaurant.

DoorDash's Own Response — The Direct Products

Restaurant Business Online documents that DoorDash's position is not entirely monolithic: "DoorDash's Drive, Storefront and Self-Delivery products, which establishments can use to help fulfill orders they generate themselves, allow establishments to keep customer data." The distinction is critical: orders generated through the DoorDash marketplace belong to DoorDash. Orders generated through the restaurant's own channels and fulfilled using DoorDash logistics can belong to the restaurant. Restaurant Business Online, restaurantbusinessonline.com, 2021

This is the architecture that gives operators a path forward: use platform logistics without using platform marketplace. Own the ordering channel, own the data, optionally outsource the delivery. The commission structure is different — typically lower for self-delivery arrangements — and the data stays with the restaurant.

The operational prescription: Build your own ordering channel. Use platforms as a logistics layer, not a customer acquisition layer. Every guest who orders through your domain is a guest record in your CRM. Every guest who orders through the marketplace is a guest record in theirs.

The Reclaim Roadmap
How to Reclaim Your Data Without Losing Your Volume
The goal is not to eliminate platforms overnight. It is to build the first-party infrastructure that gradually reduces dependence — while keeping the volume that platforms currently drive. The migration is a 12–18 month operation, not a week-one switch.
Phase 1 — Capture Before You Convert (Weeks 1–4)

Start collecting data at every in-venue touchpoint before attempting to pull guests away from platforms. The in-venue guest is already yours. Install QR opt-ins at every table, receipt prompt email capture at POS, and SMS join prompts on all tabletop materials. Goal: capture contact information for 15–25% of in-venue guests per shift, building toward an owned list of 500+ active contacts within 90 days.

Phase 2 — Build the Direct Channel (Months 2–4)

Deploy a first-party ordering page — not a marketplace listing. Your domain, your menu, your branding. The goal is to have a URL you own that generates orders you can fulfill and guest data you keep. According to Qu's 2025 data, 40% of brands are already moving in this direction because the first-party channel produces better margin and complete data ownership. Qu 2025 State of Digital Report, qubeyond.com

Phase 3 — Incentivize Migration (Months 6–12)

Run a deliberate campaign to shift existing platform customers to your direct channel. The conversion offer must be compelling: "Order direct and get 10% off every order permanently" is a structural incentive that changes the math for guests who use platforms primarily for the ordering convenience, not the platform loyalty. Restaurant Business Online documents that this conversion is hard — but the operators who run consistent campaigns over 6–12 months report measurable gains. Restaurant Business Online, restaurantbusinessonline.com, Nov. 24, 2025

The 90-Day Data Reclaim Plan
WEEK 1 — INSTALL QR OPT-INS
Every table, every receipt, every bar menu. Text-to-join and email capture at point of transaction. Cost: near zero. Capture rate: 15–25% per shift.
WEEK 2–4 — LANDING PAGE
Your own ordering URL. Link from all social profiles, Google Business, email signature, and physical menus. Make it easier to find than the platform listing.
MONTH 2–4 — FIRST EMAIL CAMPAIGN
Send your first owned-list email. "You're on the inside now — here's what's new and why you should come back." Measure open rate, click rate, and visit attribution.
MONTH 5-12 — MIGRATION CAMPAIGN
Promote direct channel to platform customers via all available physical touchpoints. Structural incentive: 10% off every direct order, permanently. Track % of orders shifting from platform to direct monthly.
The target: 20% of delivery orders through first-party channels within 12 months. At $50 avg order and 300 orders/month, that is 60 orders generating complete guest data and 12–30% better margin. Every percentage point of platform migration is permanent profit recovery.

Chapter Summary & What Comes Next
Customer Data Is the Advantage
Location used to be the moat. Being on the right corner guaranteed traffic. In the platform era, customer data has replaced location as the primary source of repeat revenue. The operator who owns the relationship doesn't need the foot traffic — they can generate it.
1
THE PLATFORMS OWN YOUR GUESTS — NOT YOU
Every standard DoorDash marketplace order produces zero customer data for the establishment. DoorDash holds 67% of US delivery market share. The data ownership battle became a legislative fight — but operators cannot wait for legislation. Source: Restaurant Business Online; Deliverect via CNBC, Feb. 2026
2
THE RESERVATION WARS HAVE EXTENDED THE CAPTURE TO DINE-IN
DoorDash's $1.2B SevenRooms acquisition, Uber Eats + OpenTable partnership, and AmEx's $400M Tock acquisition mean the same platforms that captured delivery data are now capturing reservation data. The data cycle is nearly complete. Source: CNBC, Feb. 25, 2026; Ben Leventhal
3
ONE EXTRA VISIT PER GUEST PER YEAR = 5–10% REVENUE GROWTH
Blaze Pizza CTO Chris Demery, cited in Qu's 2025 State of Digital Report, documents that even one additional annual visit per guest translates to 5–10% revenue growth — achievable only through the owned data that makes re-engagement possible. Source: Qu 2025 State of Digital Report, qubeyond.com
4
THE MIGRATION STARTS WITH ONE QR CODE AND A 90-DAY PLAN
First-party ordering, QR opt-ins at tab close, SMS text-to-join, and email receipt capture are all available today. The conversion from platform to direct channel is hard but measurable. The operators who run consistent 12-month campaigns report meaningful percentage shifts. Source: Qu 2025; Restaurant Business Online, Nov. 2025
