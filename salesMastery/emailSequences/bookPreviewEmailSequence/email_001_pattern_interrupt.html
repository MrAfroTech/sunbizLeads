The Hidden Mistake
You're Solving
The Wrong Problem

"More offers won't fix a venue where getting a drink takes 14 minutes. You're not losing guests because you don't have enough deals. You're losing them because the experience fights them at every turn."

Here's what I see at almost every venue I talk to: they're throwing promotions at a friction problem.

Two-for-one on Tuesday. Early bird tickets. A loyalty punch card. All of it layered on top of a guest experience that's quietly bleeding revenue every single night — slow lines, confusing entry, concession staff who don't know what's in stock, a point-of-sale system that wasn't built for a crowd of 8,000 people moving fast.

The offers aren't the problem. The friction is the problem. And until you fix the friction, the offers are just noise on top of a broken system.

I spent years watching this pattern repeat. A venue invests in a big marketing push, attendance goes up, and then the reviews come back with the same feedback they always get: "The lines were insane." "Couldn't find the bathroom." "Took forever to get a beer." The next promotion has to work even harder to get those guests back.

The estimated spend left on the table per guest when friction — long waits, abandoned carts, slow service — interrupts the decision to buy. That's before you count the guests who don't come back.
Composite: Hospitality Technology, NRA, PwC Smart Venue Report

That number isn't a guess. It's built from real data on dwell time, average transaction value, and the point at which a guest who was going to spend money decides it's not worth the wait. At a venue doing 5,000 guests a night, that's $90,000 to $135,000 in potential revenue — per event — that never gets captured.

Not because the guests didn't want to spend it. Because your system made it too hard.

Two Ways To Think About It
The Real Comparison
❌ What most venues do

Layer more promotions, more marketing, more "engagement" on top of a guest experience that already has 6 invisible friction points between entry and the first purchase.

✓ What smart operators do

Map where guests slow down, stop spending, or give up — then systematically remove those moments. Revenue follows the path of least resistance.

The operators who win long-term aren't the ones running the most creative promotions. They're the ones whose venues feel effortless. Guests don't notice great operations — they just have a good time and want to come back. They notice bad operations immediately, and they tell everyone.

That's the whole thesis of Chaos Mastery. Not that technology or marketing is the answer. It's that the guest experience is the revenue strategy — and most venues have never looked at it that way.
