The gap between your prep work and what the venue actually knows
THE VENUE DROPPED THE BALL
"You spend weeks collecting what your clients care about. Dietary needs. VIP guests. Drink preferences. The venue gets a one-page brief and a prayer."
3 things you'll take from this
1
The handoff is where your reputation goes to die.
When floor staff don't know that Table 4 is a VIP or that the MOB has a shellfish allergy, the guest blames the experience — and the experience is yours. Not the venue's. Yours. The brief you handed over didn't survive contact with a busy Friday night.
2
Guest intelligence at the venue level now exists — and planners can require it.
A QR-based system that surfaces returning guest preferences to staff the moment they scan in isn't a future thing. Planners who are already speccing it into their venue requirements are running events where staff actually know things — before the guest has to repeat themselves.
3
One dropped preference doesn't cost you one guest. It costs you the re-book.
Your business runs on referrals and repeat clients. When something goes wrong at the venue level, the client doesn't call the venue to complain. They just quietly book someone else next time. The cost of a missed preference isn't the moment — it's everything that follows it.
The venues you recommend are a direct reflection of your judgment. Guest intelligence gives you a way to close the gap between what you know about your clients and what the venue delivers on the day. Next issue: how to use this as a qualifier when evaluating venues — and what to ask for.
