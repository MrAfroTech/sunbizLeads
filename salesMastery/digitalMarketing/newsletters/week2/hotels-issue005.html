Mobile Order = Higher Check — The Ancillary Revenue Suppressed by Your Request Flow
YOUR GUESTS WANT TO SPEND MORE DURING THEIR STAY.
YOUR SERVICE REQUEST PROCESS IS STOPPING THEM.

"Hotel guests who can order in-stay services digitally spend more per stay and report higher satisfaction. The mechanism is the same every time: remove the friction from the request and the request happens more often."

Hotels have always had an ancillary revenue problem. The room rate is the easy part — guests commit to it at booking. But the restaurant cover, the spa treatment, the in-room dining order, the minibar upgrade, the late checkout — those are all decisions guests make in the moment, under the conditions the hotel creates for them.

The research on digital ordering across hospitality consistently shows the same pattern: when guests can make requests digitally — without navigating a phone menu, without waiting on hold, without feeling like they're imposing on someone — they make those requests more often and they spend more per stay. The per-transaction lift from digital ordering in F&B settings runs 15 to 29%. In a hotel environment, where the same guest is making multiple ancillary purchase decisions across a multi-day stay, the compounding effect is significant.

The 70% of hotel guests who say they prefer app or kiosk interactions for service requests aren't asking for less hospitality. They're asking for easier access to the hospitality you're already offering. That's the gap most properties are sitting in right now — services that exist, revenue that's available, and a request process that's just friction-heavy enough to suppress the ask.

WHY DIGITAL IN-STAY ORDERING STRUCTURALLY RAISES REVENUE PER GUEST
Four Reasons Easier Access to Hotel Services Means More Revenue Per Stay.
01
THE REQUEST RATE
Guests Who Can Request Digitally Make More Requests. The Math Is That Simple.
A guest who wants room service at 10 PM faces a decision: pick up the phone, navigate a menu system, wait on hold, and hope the process is fast. Or skip it and order delivery. Digital in-room ordering removes that entire cost from the guest's decision. Request rates for in-room dining, housekeeping, and amenity bookings consistently climb when the request path becomes a tap rather than a phone call.
02
THE BROWSE LIFT
Digital Menus Surface Options Guests Didn't Know You Had. That Drives Ancillary Spend.
A guest who calls room service orders what they already know. A guest who opens a digital in-room menu discovers the late-night special, the curated cocktail menu, the breakfast pre-order option. The same browse effect that drives 15 to 29% higher per-transaction spend in F&B settings applies to hotel ancillary ordering — because the mechanism is identical: remove time pressure and expand the menu a guest actually sees.
03
THE GEN Z SIGNAL
82% of Gen Z Hotel Guests Prefer Digital Interactions. They're Also Your Fastest-Growing Segment.
Gen Z hotel travelers don't just prefer digital check-in — they prefer the entire hotel service interaction to be available without a phone call. A property that routes in-stay requests, F&B orders, and amenity bookings through a digital interface isn't just satisfying a preference. It's removing the friction that suppresses ancillary spend for your highest-growth guest demographic.
04
THE SATISFACTION LOOP
Guests Who Spend More Per Stay on Ancillaries Also Rate Their Stays Higher. Friction Suppresses Both.
The correlation between ancillary spend and guest satisfaction isn't accidental. Guests who use the spa, order in-room dining, and book a late checkout have a richer stay experience — and they rate it accordingly. Removing the friction from making those requests doesn't just raise revenue. It raises the review score that determines your booking conversion for the next 12 months.

WHERE THE IN-STAY DIGITAL ORDERING LIFT IS SITTING IN YOUR PROPERTY RIGHT NOW
Four Revenue Moments Suppressed by Friction in the Current Request Flow.
IN-ROOM DINING
In-room dining is the highest-friction ancillary in most hotels — and therefore the one most suppressed by the phone-call request model. Digital in-room ordering that mirrors a restaurant-quality menu experience has consistently shown the same browse-and-add behavior that drives 15 to 29% lift in F&B settings. The food hasn't changed. The process of ordering it has.
SPA & AMENITIES
Spa and amenity bookings are almost entirely impulse-driven for most hotel guests. The guest who decides to book a treatment on Saturday morning is making that decision in a 20-second window. If the booking path is a front-desk call, most of those impulses don't convert. If it's a QR tap from the room, most of them do.
F&B UPSELL
Hotel restaurants and bars lose the most ancillary revenue to friction. A guest who enjoyed dinner last night will return tonight — if returning feels easy. Digital reservations, digital menus, and in-app hotel dining offers convert the guest who would have skipped it because calling felt like too much coordination.
LATE CHECKOUT
Late checkout is one of the most consistently requested ancillary services — and most hotels make it harder than it needs to be to ask for. A digital request flow that lets guests extend their stay in two taps converts that desire into revenue. A phone-call-required process means most guests pack up and leave because the ask felt like too much work.

The revenue from ancillary services in a hotel doesn't come from convincing guests to spend money they weren't planning to spend. It comes from removing the friction between an intention they already have and the act of following through on it. Digital in-stay ordering does exactly that — and the per-stay revenue difference between a property that makes it easy and one that doesn't is measurable, consistent, and compounding.
