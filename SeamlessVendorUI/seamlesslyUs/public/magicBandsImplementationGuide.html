THE FRICTION ECONOMY

Most venues don’t have a demand problem. They have a friction problem.

Every long line, delayed drink, crowded concession area, or confusing ordering workflow quietly reduces guest spending.

Not because guests don’t want to buy — but because the purchasing experience becomes mentally exhausting.

In hospitality, convenience directly affects revenue velocity.

The average guest makes emotional spending decisions. If ordering feels difficult, uncertain, crowded, or slow, spending behavior changes immediately.

The result:

• Lower drink frequency
• Fewer impulse purchases
• Abandoned concession visits
• Reduced premium spending
• Earlier departures
• Lower guest satisfaction

The core principle:

Guests spend more when spending feels easier.

THE PSYCHOLOGY OF WAITING
Programmatic · Financial · Psychological · Sociological
"People hate waiting. But they hate waiting when they don't know how long they're going to wait even more."
PROGRAMMATIC
67%
One stadium study reported a 67% drop in scan engagement when a MagicBands was not visible long enough to act on. Visibility is not a design preference. It is a conversion variable. The system only works when the guest can see it, read it, and scan it before the moment passes.
FINANCIAL
$0
Every minute a guest spends in a concession line is a minute they are not spending money anywhere else in your venue. Abandoned purchase behavior — guests who walk away from a line before ordering — represents revenue that never enters your POS system and never shows up in your reporting. It is invisible loss. And it compounds every game.
PSYCHOLOGICAL
8 mins
Research consistently shows that unoccupied wait time feels nearly three times longer than occupied wait time. A guest standing in a line with no information, no progress signal, and no alternative feels the wait at a psychological multiplier. That feeling transfers directly to their perception of your venue — not the line. Your venue.
SOCIOLOGICAL
Group Effect
Waiting is a social experience. When one person in a group abandons a line, the group often follows. When one person in a group scans and orders from their seat, the people around them ask how they did it. Frictionless ordering is socially contagious in both directions. The question is which direction your current infrastructure is spreading.
