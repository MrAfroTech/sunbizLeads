Why This Matters
Most hotels are solving the wrong problem.
The instinct is to optimize for occupancy. The data says the real margin lives in what guests spend after they drop their bags. Those are two different revenue models — and only one scales without more rooms.
🏨
Occupancy isn't the finish line
Only 28% of guests use on-site dining without a prompt. The room is booked — the ancillary revenue window is wide open and mostly ignored.
💸
Margin lives on property
Better service flow across dining, amenities, and room service moves total revenue without adding a single new booking. Most GMs don't model that number daily.
🔄
Direct rebook beats the OTA
Guests who feel unrecognized during a stay go back to the OTA. Guests who feel known rebook direct — and spend more on the return visit.
📊
Contextual touchpoints compound
Dining reminders, amenity offers, and checkout options each move the needle independently. Properties that connect all three recover up to 45% of missed ancillary revenue.

Guests who feel unrecognized during a stay are significantly less likely to rebook directly — they go back to the OTA.

The Speed Standard Points 8 – 9 · What good actually looks like
POINT 8 · CHECK-IN SPEED
Check-in speed has a direct, measurable impact on how much a guest spends during their stay. The faster the friction is removed, the sooner revenue begins.
Speed isn't about rushing the guest. It's about removing friction from the parts of the experience that don't need to be slow — so ancillary offers land while guests are still deciding.
Source: Hotel check-in speed and on-property spend analysis
POINT 9 · THE FIRST HOUR
Guests who check in within 5 minutes and receive one personalized touchpoint within the first hour report higher satisfaction and higher on-property spend.
Ten minutes post-check-in is the conversion window. Five minutes to get them in the room. One touchpoint in the first hour. That's the operating standard the data supports.
Source: Hotel check-in and first-hour engagement research
